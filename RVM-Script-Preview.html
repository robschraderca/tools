Explaining "Validate"

I want to take a moment to explain that first word "Validate" — because this is going to do the heavy lifting for you with the types of debt you have. Your debts all have a few things in common: they're private, unsecured, and not tied to any collateral.
To properly explain validation, I want to touch on two laws that are really the foundation of the entire program — specifically the Fair Credit Reporting Act and the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act.
These laws outline what a creditor can and cannot do when collecting a debt from a borrower. At their core, these laws are designed to prevent false and inaccurate reporting — but they're also the exact laws that define what a company needs to have in order to even prove a debt is owed in the first place.
The way they prove it is through what's called triple-verified debt — they need your date of birth, your Social Security number, and the original signed contract. Nothing out of the ordinary — that's the exact information used to open these accounts to begin with.
But what most people don't realize is that when a debt is sold or transferred in any way, there are multiple privacy laws in place that prevent most of that information from carrying over.
The obvious one is your Social — nobody can sell your Social Security number for profit. And you would know better than anyone that after a debt was sold, you never sat down and signed any new paperwork, agreements, or contracts with the third-party collector. That's already two of the three required proof elements missing.
This is where we step in. We are partnered with Ironwood Law Group. We demand legal proof from these creditors — proof we know they won't have. Our attorneys send Cease and Desist letters to stop all collection attempts while we work on your behalf.
Once we prove the debt invalid, it becomes uncollectable, and we request full removal of the tradeline from your credit report. Not just the balance — the entire history of delinquency is expunged. So no one in the future can see you even had these debts, let alone didn't pay them back in full.
So this is a true credit report RESET. Does that make sense?
